About the Green Bangle Movement

Green Bangle Movement (GBM) was born out of a simple question: what if coastal protection, women’s work and climate education could be woven into the same circle? Starting from a university campus and spreading into nearby coastal areas, GBM brings students, women and local leaders together around nurseries, mangroves and palmyra palms.

The “bangle” in our name carries memory — of women’s hands, shared labour and everyday strength. Our green bangles are living ones: rings of trees, shorelines of care, and networks of women who hold communities through uncertainty.

Meet Our Founder

Dr. P. Mosae Selvakumar

Founder & Lead Mentor, Green Bangle Movement
Director of Environmental Sciences Program, Asian University for Women

With a lifelong passion for sustainable development, traditional ecological knowledge, and community-centered innovation, Dr. Selvakumar envisioned the Green Bangle Movement as a bridge between science, women’s empowerment, and ecological restoration. His leadership has shaped GBM into a community-driven initiative rooted in hope, resilience, and hands-on environmental action.

Our Journey

GBM’s journey has grown through festivals, workshops, nurseries and long days in the field with mud on our hands and hope in our hearts. Each step connects students, women and communities around coastal resilience.

Phase 1 · Awareness & community bonding

Palmyra Fruit Festival – celebrating local knowledge & green culture

The journey began with joy, colour and community spirit. The Palmyra Fruit Festival at AUW created a bridge between environmental knowledge and cultural celebration. Students, faculty and mentors came together to reconnect with indigenous plants, traditional uses and the importance of protecting local biodiversity.

Phase 2 · Education & capacity building

Environmental journalism – learning to amplify green voices

GBM collaborated with experts to empower young women with tools to tell impactful environmental stories. This workshop strengthened climate awareness, storytelling skills and the confidence to advocate for sustainable solutions in their communities.

Phase 3 · Skills for sustainability

Hands-on nursery training – growing skills, confidence & leadership

Women from local communities received training in sapling care, soil preparation and nursery management. These circles became a foundation for community-led environmental stewardship and new livelihood opportunities.

Phase 4 · Coastal restoration

Mangrove plantation at Guliakhali – protecting our fragile coastline

Students and volunteers worked side by side to plant mangroves at Guliakhali, a vulnerable coastal area. The young trees support erosion control, biodiversity recovery and long-term climate resilience for coastal communities.

Phase 5 · Expanding green footprints

Palmyra sapling plantation – reviving climate-smart heritage trees

GBM volunteers planted Palmyra saplings, known for their heat resilience, deep roots and cultural significance. Each sapling is a promise of shade, water retention and long-term ecological restoration.

Phase 6 · Youth engagement

Green leadership – learning through muddy hands and shared work

Young volunteers immersed themselves in planting, soil work and ecological activities. Muddy hands became a symbol of connection with the land and a shared commitment to climate resilience.

Phase 7 · Landscape restoration

Planting in hilly terrains – rebuilding fragile ecosystems

Volunteers planted native species across hilly and degraded landscapes around Chattogram. These plants help stabilise slopes, restore habitats and support long-term ecosystem health in vulnerable regions.

Phase 8 · Community outreach

Sapling walk – spreading green hope, one household at a time

Volunteers walked through surrounding communities carrying saplings, sharing knowledge and encouraging families to care for trees at home. Each plant is a small, living bond between GBM and the communities we walk with.
